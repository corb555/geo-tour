Jemez Lineament

A chain of volcanic activity stretches diagonally across New Mexico: the Jemez Lineament.

This is a deep subduction scar formed 1.7 B years ago. When an ancient island arc collided with North America, it trapped water-rich minerals deep within the Earth's mantle. This "hydrous" rock melts more easily than the surrounding dry rock, creating a magma source waiting to be used.

Where this intersects the deep fracture of the Rio Grande Rift, the crust is pulled thin creating an area that has allowed magma to break the surface repeatedly.

This line of fire strings together some of the state's most prominent landmarks:

  • Valles Caldera. While this 13-mile-wide depression appears today as a peaceful, grassy bowl, its vast circular form reveals its true nature: a collapsed "supervolcano".
  • Mount Taylor: This massive, eroded stratovolcano rises over 11,300 feet, towering over the high desert.
  • El Malpais: A landscape of young, black basaltic lava flows and tube systems known as "The Badlands."
