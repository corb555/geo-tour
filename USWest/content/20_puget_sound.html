Glaciation

The topography of the Seattle region is also a product of the Puget Lobe of the Cordilleran Ice Sheet. During the glacial maximum (approx. 17,000 years ago), ice over 3,000 feet thick occupied this basin.

The ice acted as an erosional engine, carving deep, U-shaped Glacial Troughs. The visible hills are drumlins and deposits of Glacial Till—unsorted sediment left by the retreating ice. This retreat also dropped massive Glacial Erratics—house-sized boulders carried from Canada and stranded in random locations throughout the region.
